Crime and Punishment — Reading Session 1

(From the Beginning Until “slip out unseen”)

As part of our English literature reading program for university students and working professionals, our team selected Fyodor Dostoevsky’s Crime and Punishment as our first novel.

This page presents:

  • A curated vocabulary list from the selected passage
  • A complete text-analysis guide
  • Questions and answers to develop deep comprehension

1. Key Vocabulary From the Passage

  1. Garret – A very small room at the top of a house, just under the roof.
  2. Lodged – Lived or stayed somewhere (usually rented).
  3. In hesitation – Acting slowly because you’re unsure or afraid.
  4. Landlady – A woman who owns the house or room you rent.
  5. Cupboard – A small storage space; here, it implies the room is tiny.
  6. Invariably – Always, every single time.
  7. Sick, frightened feeling – A fear that tightens your stomach.
  8. Scowl – An angry or unhappy facial expression.
  9. Hopelessly in debt – Owing money and unable to pay it back.
  10. Abject – Extremely weak, miserable, without confidence.
  11. Overstrained – Mentally overworked or stressed.
  12. Irritable – Easily annoyed or angered.
  13. Verging (on) – Almost becoming something.
  14. Hypochondria – Fear of having illnesses without reason.
  15. Absorbed in himself – Lost in his own thoughts.
  16. Isolated – Alone or separated from others.
  17. Practical importance – Everyday responsibilities or tasks.
  18. Trivial – Unimportant or small.
  19. Irrelevant – Not connected to what matters.
  20. Pestering – Repeatedly annoying someone.
  21. Prevaricate – Avoid telling the truth; speak confusingly.
  22. Lie – Say something untrue.
  23. Creep (down the stairs) – Move quietly so no one notices.
  24. Slip out unseen – Leave secretly without being noticed.

2. Text Analysis

Below you’ll find structured comprehension activities with three questions per level, each followed by model answers.

Level 1

  • 1. Where does the young man live?
    He lives in a garret, a small attic room in a five-storied house in S. Place.
  • 2. Who lives on the floor below him?
    His landlady, who provides meals and services.
  • 3. What feeling does he experience when he passes the kitchen?
    He feels sick, frightened, ashamed, and anxious.

Level 2

  • 1. Why does he avoid the landlady?
    Because he owes her money and fears her questions or complaints.
  • 2. How is his garret described, and what does it reveal?
    It is “more like a cupboard than a room,” suggesting poverty and isolation.
  • 3. What does his fear of meeting her tell us about him?
    He is anxious and socially withdrawn, though not cowardly.

Level 3

  • 1. If you were in his place in debt, how might you behave differently?
    Possible answer: communicate openly or repay part of the debt.
  • 2. Imagine another person living in such a garret—what might their life feel like?
    Cramped, lonely, stressful, uncomfortable.
  • 3. How could his fear affect other relationships?
    He may avoid people and become socially anxious.

Level 4

  • 1. How does Dostoevsky use the city to reflect his state of mind?
    The heat, smells, and crowded streets mirror his anxiety.
  • 2. What contrast exists between his fear and the landlady’s behavior?
    She behaves normally; his fear is internal and psychological.
  • 3. What do small details reveal about social pressure?
    They show constant scrutiny and judgment.

Level 5

  • 1. Is his fear reasonable?
    Reasonable due to debt, but exaggerated since she isn’t dangerous.
  • 2. How effectively is tension created?
    Very effectively, through vivid setting and internal thoughts.
  • 3. Does the garret description create sympathy?
    Yes — it highlights his isolation and poverty.

Level 6

  • 1. Rewrite the scene from the landlady’s perspective.
    Example: She thinks he is strange, unreliable, or troubled.
  • 2. Imagine a modern version of this man.
    He might avoid crowded streets, worry about bills, and feel stressed.
  • 3. Write a diary entry from his perspective.
    “July 3rd. The heat is unbearable. Passing the kitchen tightens my chest...”
